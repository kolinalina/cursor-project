## Post 1
**Date:** 2026-04-25

🚨 We need to get comfortable using own-site business impact proxy signals in AI search measurement.

None of these metrics prove AI influence on their own. The value comes from reading them as a set and asking whether the pattern is consistent with an AI-driven story.

There is one survey question that earns its place, and I recommend adding it to your signup flow, demo request, or post-trial onboarding:

> Before signing up, did you come across \[brand\] in an AI assistant or AI search experience, such as ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or Google's AI features?

Options:
- Yes
- No
- Not sure

Place it after the core signup fields, not before. Make it optional. Keep it to one question with no follow-up (once it turns into a mini-survey, completion rates collapse).

Why it matters disproportionately:
- A rising "Yes" rate among users attributed to Direct or Branded Organic is one of the strongest first-party proxies that AI influence exists beyond what analytics can directly observe.
- Users who arrive via branded search with a high "Yes" rate are often invisible AI influence. They are attributed to Organic in GA4 but might not have searched your brand without an AI mention.
- Users who arrive via Direct with a high "Yes" rate are often the mobile-ChatGPT copy-paste cohort, which GA4 attribution is blind to.
- Users who arrive via the AI Search channel but answer "No" may include misattributed traffic (agency traffic, internal team members, secondary clicks).

## Post 2
**Date:** 2026-04-24

📊 A 3-layer framework to measure AI presence, readiness, and business impact: redefining metrics for the AI search era.

In this new guide, I cover how the old organic search measurement model-built largely around rankings, clicks, and sessions-is becoming less sufficient on its own in an AI search environment.

I propose a structured way to measure, diagnose, and prioritize in an environment where observability is partial, platform behavior differs, and influence often extends beyond directly attributable clicks.

The 3 layers of AI search success:

1. **Presence**  
   Whether the brand appears in the AI answers that matter, how it is represented, cited, linked, and recommended.  
   Replaces traffic-only thinking with visibility and representation measurement.  
   **Visibility KPIs:** optimization and monitoring.

2. **Readiness**  
   Whether the structural conditions needed for stronger visibility are in place.  
   Explains why visibility is weak, strong, or unstable-the diagnostic layer.  
   **Diagnostic KPIs:** diagnosis and prioritization.

3. **Business Impact**  
   Whether visibility is translating into measurable value, using observed, proxy, and modeled signals.  
   Connects AI search activity to commercial outcomes without overclaiming attribution.  
   **Outcome KPIs:** executive reporting and decision-making.

The point is not to run three disconnected audits. It is that:
- Presence tells you where the brand appears.
- Readiness tells you why it looks that way.
- Business Impact tells you whether that visibility creates measurable value.

Each layer hands off a hypothesis to the next. That is what turns three reports into one diagnostic.

## Post 3
**Date:** 2026-04-24

👀 Google Search Central Live Toronto slides and summary by [Jean-Christophe Chouinard](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jeanchristophechouinard/) 👇

Highlights:
- **The indexing and quality bar:** AI has lowered the barrier for content creation, forcing Google to raise the bar for what gets indexed.
- **Crawl vs. index:** "Crawled - currently not indexed" is rarely a technical rendering issue; it is usually a quality signal, canonical/duplicate issue, or sometimes 404/redirect/robots.txt issues.
- **Scaled content abuse:** This algorithm may explain AI-generated content traffic drops, rather than AI usage itself. Google is not against AI per se, but applies safeguards against scaled content and what gets indexed.
- **Google-Extended and fanouts:** Blocking does not negatively impact presence in AI Overviews or AI Mode. Blocking Google-Extended is less effective than many think, because Google can still use indexed site data in fanouts to generate AI answers.
